Climbing Mt. CSE 125

Last edited: January 1, 2021

Helpful tips, takeaways, and links from a former student and TA.

"The goal of CSE 125 is to experience the design and implementation of a large, complex software system in large groups. . . To make the class exciting as well as challenging, the project is a distributed, real-time, 3D, multiplayer game of each group's design."

CSE 125 2020 Demo Flyer

1. Meet up often

This might seem like an absolute no-brainer, but it is an incredibly important starting step that jumpstarted many teams, especially when the course was remote.  According to a Google research study conducted on +180 active teams at Google, the top 2 factors that contribute to a team's success are psychological safety & dependability. If you're curious about what makes teams successful, I would highly recommend reading the study to learn more! Just to summarize, each member on the team should feel comfortable sharing new ideas to the team and asking each other for help. Team members should also be able to trust in each other to complete their deliverables.  From my experiences, I found that meeting up with my team, especially outside of course/development hours, helped bring everyone on the team closer together. Many times, my team would get lunch in Price Center, study in the CSE dungeons, play online games, and attend school events together. We had even planned a group DMV trip to register for our Real IDs; a trip that unfortunately ended up taking over 6 hours because I managed to lock myself out of my own car (and according to my team, I'll never live this down). Our team had a lot of face-to-face interactions that bonded each of the team members closer together. Since I was able to get to closer to my team, bouncing new ideas, proposing last minute changes, and even simply asking for help was easy to do. Everyone on the team felt connected with one another and on the same page.  In a remote setting, it might be a bit more difficult to get this same level of cohesion within the team. I would recommend setting up a reoccurring team lunch just to hang out and chitchat, play some online games together, and consider even setting up a Discord server for the team. 2. Game Design

One of my favorite aspects of CSE 125 is that each team has the freedom to design any game they would like - under the constraints that they are able to build the game within 10 weeks. In the past years, teams have built their own versions of first person shooters, tower defense games, battle arena games, boss battle games, games with imbalanced teams, games with a building stage and then a fighting stage… the list goes on and on.  Reference existing games Many teams like to reference mechanics from existing games as starting point for designing their own games. Looking at the games from 2020, Cyber City was a cyberpunk, tower defense, battle arena game that had a similar minion and turret system based off of League of Legends. Grow Away, a plant-based, tower defense game, took concepts from Plants vs. Zombies, OverCooked, and Bloons Tower Defense. Create something new Another popular path is to come up with a new mechanics and use these mechanics as a focal point for the game. Gaia was a game that had a fairly simple graphics but a unique terrain manipulation feature. Each team consisted of 2 players, one player controlling a sphere that can roll around and collect coins, and another player that could raise and lower mountains to help their sphere traverse the terrain easier or block the other team's sphere.  There are very little restrictions on what type of game your team can build. I would highly recommend blue-skying some potential game ideas and later discussing these ideas with the professor and TA.

3. Program Management

One unique aspect of CSE 125 compared to other CSE courses is that the class is very unstructured. There aren't reoccurring lectures that you need to go to or bi-weekly homework that you need to submit. Therefore all of the teams decisions, planning, and deadlines are established by the team as well.  To ensure your team understands what needs to be done each week, I would highly recommend that your team invests a bit of time get week into a project management. During this weekly meeting, teammates can discuss what they have worked on so far, the tasks that they need to finish by next weeks meeting, and if there are any blockers in their way. Having a project management board can also serve to keep a general timeline of the project and see what is left to be done.  Here are a few Kanban boards to help you get started!
