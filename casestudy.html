LinkedIn: Event Groups

Efficiently coordinating LinkedIn members who are attending the same events.

Collaborators

Roshan Fernando, Prabhav Khandelwal
Advised by Professor Philip Guo

My Role

UX Designer / Researcher
Delivered: Primary research, secondary research, concept development, low-fi/hi-fi wireframes, team management, case study content strategy

Problem Space:

There are over 575 million users on LinkedIn and over 260 million active monthly users. This huge network is shared by both recruiters and opportunists seeking avenues to enhance their professional careers.

Likewise, there are hundreds of large national professional conferences each year, including the Grace Hopper Conference, the National Society of Black Engineers Conference, and IEEE conferences. In addition, there are thousands of small- and medium-sized networking events daily, both internal to companies and across firms.

As attendees of conferences and young professionals, we struggle to maintain relationships with our LinkedIn connections. We don’t see them on a regular basis, leading to awkward re-introductions and “not sure if you remember me”s when we need to contact them again.

As recruiters of conferences, we struggle to quickly and efficiently organize attendees into target groups. On LinkedIn, we currently can't verify to see who is going to a particular conference. Instead, we search for events by their hashtags and assume those that use the hashtag are also attending the event.

Challenge:

LinkedIn users need to know who will be at the professional conferences and networking events they attend, in order to rekindle old connections, strengthen current connections, and discover new connections.

Target Audience:

We’ve decided to focus on attendees of professional conferences, networking events, and recruiting events as our target audience.

Gabriella, 26

Recruiter at BookFace

Gabriella attends multiple professional conference events each year to hire new talent. Prior to attending these conferences, she would like to know which of her connections are verified as attendees to the conference in order to plan on-site interviews at the event.

David, 24

Software Engineer at InLinked

David has attended a few professional conferences in the past. As a semi-avid user of InLinked and other forms of social media, he is interested in seeing who else in her network is attending the conference and potentially setting up meet-ups during the event.

Research Findings:

Data from our Google Forms Survey - 28 respondents

  • 89% of LinkedIn users connect with different people on LinkedIn than they connect with on other social media
  • 90% of conference attendees don’t know which of their LinkedIn connections will also be there
  • 33% of conference attendees focus more than half of their time on re-connecting with previous friends/connections

User Flow:

The attendee workflow is designed for attendees of conferences and professional events, who want to know which connections are attending the same event, and what new connections can they find. Through this workflow, they can both find new connections from attendees, and view which of their connections are attending.

The recruiter workflow is designed for company recruiters who attend conferences to find potential candidates. Through the workflow, the recruiter can see who in their network is guaranteed to be at the conference, and also discover new candidates who will also be at the conference. Furthermore, they can filter and segment people at the event in order to find LinkedIn users that would be a great fit for their firm. Through the workflow, they can discover who will be at the conference, and utilize LinkedIn’s messenger features to schedule in-person meetings at the conference.

Ideation:

Our goal is to extend the LinkedIn Events feature in a way that encourages in-person interaction at conferences and professional events. We started by first drawing out a few concept ideas. These served as a basis for shaping our idea into something concrete and gave our team a talking point to discuss certain features.

Paper Prototypes:

From our initial UI sketches, we agreed that the most effective way to help increase connections for both attendees and recruiters at professional events was to redesign the current LinkedIn events platform and extend a user-created group feature. Therefore, attendees could create groups with previous connections who were also attending the event and recruiters can organize targetted attendees into specific groups.

High Fidelity Mockups:

Our prototype implements a combination of the recruiter and attendee flows in order to maintain an ease of use for both parties. The first row of screens showcases a complete events view, an indiviual event view, and a create event view. This redesign contains a dedicated page for viewing events on LinkedIn and illustrates a clear method to view attendees of an event. The second row of screens showcases how to create groups from the attendees pool and an entire group view. This functionality is open to both recruiters and attendees in order to organize the attendees pool to their specific needs.

User Testing

Our user tests reveal very important insights and problems with our original design, primarily with the functionality and navigation aspects. Users were initially confused when viewing both the groups and attendees pages side by side, when trying to figure out how to add attendees into groups, and how to transition out of the create group modal.

Before

After

We wanted to differentiate the layout of the groups page from the layout of the attendees page. On the left hand side, the original group page has a very similar layout to the attendees page; each group is its own row and joining the group is the same button as adding an attendee to a group. On the right hand side, each group is now laid out in a tile-based format. We removed the adding group functionality from the tile screen, because we believe joining a group should be after entering into that individual group page. From user testing, we also found this tile-based format makes differentiating groups more visually clear and intuitve.

Key Changes:

  • Tile-based format and cover photos for more interpretability between groups
  • +Create New button integrated into tile based format

Before

After

On the left hand side, users would add attendees into their groups by clicking on their profile picture. To finalize the group, users would then click on the +Create Group button on the top left. From user testing, we found that the functionality of this design was confusing and unintuitive since it deviated from the rest of the LinkedIn style standards. In our updated design on the right hand side, we added a + button to each of the attendees and shifted the create group button to the bottom to symbolize when the group owner wants to finalize the group.

Key Changes:

  • Adding filters UI to sort attendees by preference
  • Changed Message icon to Plus icon
  • Added `+Create Group` button to the bottom

Before

After

On the left hand side, our create group modal has a `Congrats!` message when a group was created and doesn't have a clear transition out of the modal. By clicking out of the modal, it is unclear whether the screen will continue allowing the group owner to add more attendees or would the screen transition to the group page. To combat this, we changed the `Congrats!` to the group name to add more clarity to what group was just recently created. We also added a `View Group` button to add a clear nagviation path out of the modal.

Key Changes:

  • Changed `Congrats!` message to the created group name
  • Added `View Group` button to allow for smooth transition out of modal

Latest Solution:

Reflection:

Building lasting connections through design

People like what they are used to. At first, we took a blue sky approach to solving this events redesign problem: the more features, the better. However, when designing our first iteration of high fidelity mockups, we neglected the importance of having clear, simple nagivation framework. Our user tests clearly showcased our design had unintutive transition buttons and a messy layout.

Working through this problem taught me the importance of having a growth mindset. By adhering to our user feedback and constantly listening for improvement, I made key minor adjustments to help enhance the overall flow of our design.

Overall, this was a great learning experience. I could not have done it without the support of my groupmates.
